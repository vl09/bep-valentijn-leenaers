# Summary & Outlook

## Summary

In this thesis, a reliable three-axis ADXL354 measurement chain was designed and validated, then used to characterise vibrations on the final cold stage of a DIY dry 4K fridge cooled by a Gifford–McMahon cryocooler.

The calibrated chain reproduces known static and dynamic accelerations. A gravity-flip test yields a sensitivity of $S = 392\,\mathrm{mV/g}$ and a zero-$g$ offset of $\bar{V}_0 = 0.897\,\mathrm{V}$, within the datasheet band of $368$–$432\,\mathrm{mV/g}$ and within $\sim 2\%$ of the typical $400\,\mathrm{mV/g}$[@adxl354_datasheet]. A benchtop mass-spring ringdown oscillates at $f = 0.995\,\mathrm{Hz}$ with $z$-channel extrema consistent with the kinematic estimate of $0.46$–$1.54\,\mathrm{g}$.

With the GM cooler off, the final-stage ASD is dominated by electrical pickup: a $50\,\mathrm{Hz}$ mains line reaching approximately $14$, $6$, and $12\,\mathrm{mg}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ on $x$, $y$, and $z$, together with a broadband floor of order $100$–$110\,\mu\mathrm{g}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ between $60$ and $90\,\mathrm{Hz}$, roughly five times the datasheet noise density of $22.5\,\mu\mathrm{g}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$. With the cooler on, a periodic thud–thud, pause, thud, pause heartbeat with period $1.00\,\mathrm{s}$ appears in the time domain. In the frequency domain this drive produces a comb of lines spaced by $\approx 1\,\mathrm{Hz}$. The strongest low-frequency cooler lines reach $1.77\,\mathrm{mg}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at $5\,\mathrm{Hz}$ and $1.67\,\mathrm{mg}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at $7\,\mathrm{Hz}$, roughly $15$–$18$ times the cooler-off floor at the same frequencies. Across $0$–$120\,\mathrm{Hz}$, the largest cooler-on peaks sit near $99\,\mathrm{Hz}$ ($41.1\,\mathrm{mg}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ on $z$) and near $71\,\mathrm{Hz}$ ($19$–$20\,\mathrm{mg}/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ on all three channels). Higher-frequency structure is axis-dependent, consistent with mode shapes of an extended cold plate. The $50\,\mathrm{Hz}$ mains line remains comparable with the cooler on and off, confirming that it is a separate electrical contribution.

A reliable three-axis ADXL354 chain can therefore characterise the acceleration spectral density on the final stage of a GM cryocooler relative to the cooler-off baseline. The cooler drive is a clear $\approx 1\,\mathrm{Hz}$ harmonic comb standing well above that baseline.

## Outlook
Microwave optomechanics relies on millikelvin environments in which phonon motion in a high-$Q$ mechanical resonator can be read out and controlled through its coupling to a superconducting microwave cavity[@steeleLabResearch]. Dry refrigerators sustain these temperatures with closed-cycle cryocoolers, but the cooler cycle is also a periodic mechanical drive. Residual acceleration at the lowest cold stage sets part of the mechanical noise floor on which that readout rests. Passive vibration isolation is the standard response: a platform suspended on springs is tuned so that its resonance lies away from the cooler line, reducing motion transmitted to the experiment[@wilkinson2025]. Before such an isolator can be judged effective, the disturbance that reaches the cold stage must be measured.

That measurement problem was the starting point of this thesis. Plates, supports, and thermal links on the final cold stage form an extended elastic structure with many modes, so the acceleration spectrum at a mount point mixes cooler harmonics with structural resonances and electrical pickup from the readout chain. This work established that a calibrated ADXL354 chain can separate the GM cooler drive from a cooler-off baseline on the final stage of a DIY dry 4K fridge, and it recorded what that drive looks like in both the time and frequency domains. The edge-mounted spectra in [](#fig-cryostat-mount) are a first map of that environment at room temperature, not yet the vibration field seen by a device at $4\,\mathrm{K}$ on an isolated experimental platform.

It should be noted that many spectral peaks remain unassigned without modal information, that the scope readout floor was not reduced to the datasheet noise density, and that the strongest cooler bursts briefly approach the $\pm 2\,\mathrm{g}$ sensor limit. Features near $\sim 2.2\,\mathrm{kHz}$ may partly reflect the ADXL354 transfer-function resonance. These limits do not undo the main result: the cooler-on comb and the axis-dependent peaks above it are reproducible mechanical structure standing clear of the cooler-off floor. They do define what the next measurements should target.

A natural continuation is to assign the unidentified peaks. A light hammer impact on the cold plate, with simultaneous audio recording on a phone microphone, could give coarse modal hints: the acoustic track often reveals ringing frequencies that are hard to read from acceleration alone when many modes overlap. The same protocol could be applied at different positions on the DIY4K plate. Comparing centre and edge mounts, as in [](#fig-cryostat-mount), would test how strongly local mode shapes change the spectrum at the point where an experiment is likely to sit.

Repeating the cooler-off and cooler-on spectroscopy at the nominal $4\,\mathrm{K}$ operating temperature is equally important. Resonance frequencies and damping of an extended copper plate depend on temperature through thermal expansion and elastic moduli, so the peaks above $\sim 70\,\mathrm{Hz}$ need not occur at the same frequencies once the stage is cold, even though the $\approx 1\,\mathrm{Hz}$ GM drive period is expected to be far less sensitive to plate temperature. Cold operation would also require a gravity flip or another reference acceleration at low temperature, because datasheet guarantees on sensitivity and offset do not extend to cryogenic use.

The DIY dry 4K fridge is a convenient commissioning platform, but SteeleLab's main optomechanical experiments run on Bluefors dilution refrigerators precooled by pulse-tube cryocoolers[@steeleLabResearch]. Literature on such systems reports a narrow-band component near $1.4\,\mathrm{Hz}$ and its harmonics[@maisonobe2018], distinct from the $\approx 1\,\mathrm{Hz}$ GM comb measured here. Mounting the same ADXL354 chain on a Bluefors cold stage would show whether that difference persists in practice. Measuring vibrations at the pulse-tube stage itself would characterise the precooler drive directly and allow comparison with spectra recorded at lower mounting spots.

The most direct test of passive isolation is to measure on a cryogenic mass-spring platform of the type developed in SteeleLab[@wilkinson2025]. Two mount points define the transfer function of interest. First, the sensor can be placed at the spring attachment interface where the isolator meets the cold stage, as shown in [](#fig-isolator-mount). That input spectrum is the disturbance the springs must reject. Second, the sensor can be mounted on the isolated copper mass itself. Comparing cooler-on spectra at the two positions quantifies how much of the $\approx 1\,\mathrm{Hz}$ comb and its harmonics survive the isolation, and whether the isolator's designed resonance $f_0$ sits where theory expects relative to the cooler line.

In conclusion, cryostat vibration limits how precisely phonon motion can be read out in microwave optomechanics, and the cooler cycle is a periodic part of that disturbance. This thesis showed that a calibrated ADXL354 chain can measure acceleration on a GM-cooled final stage relative to a cooler-off baseline. The spectra recorded here are room-temperature commissioning data on one fridge at one mount point. They are enough to justify carrying the same chain forward: to colder plate temperatures, to other cryostats, and onto the cryogenic mass-spring platforms that SteeleLab uses to shield sensitive devices from this drive.
